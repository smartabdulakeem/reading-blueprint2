14.1
What Punctuation Does

Punctuation does for writing what breathing, pausing, and intonation do for speech. It tells the reader when to pause, when to stop, how to group ideas, and what kind of emphasis or emotion is intended.

A sentence without punctuation is like music without rests — technically possible, but exhausting and often impossible to understand.

The Big Idea
Punctuation Is Voice on the Page
When you read aloud, punctuation tells your voice exactly what to do:

Period → full stop, voice drops.  Comma → brief pause, stay level.
Question Mark → voice rises at end.  Exclamation Mark → voice intensifies.
Colon → pause with anticipation.  Dash → noticeable pivot pause.

14.3 — The Oxford Comma
The Comma That Prevents Confusion

The Oxford comma (also called the serial comma) is the final comma in a list, placed before the word "and" or "or". Its use is debated — but in academic, professional, and formal writing it is strongly recommended because it prevents ambiguity.
