2.1

The Two Great Families

The 26 letters of the alphabet are divided into two families based on how the sound is made in your mouth: Vowels and Consonants. Every word in the English language must contain at least one vowel. This is not a rule someone invented — it is a fact about how the human mouth produces language.

2.2

Vowels: The Open Sounds

There are 5 primary vowel letters: A, E, I, O, U. When you make a vowel sound, notice something important: your mouth is open and no part of your mouth blocks the flow of air. The sound flows freely outward. This is the defining physical characteristic of a vowel.

The letter Y sometimes acts as a vowel — you will learn exactly when in Phase 2.

Physical rule: Say "ahhh." Feel how open your mouth is, how freely the sound flows. That openness is what makes it a vowel sound.
2.3

Consonants: The Shaped Sounds

All 21 remaining letters are consonants. When you make a consonant sound, some part of your mouth shapes or restricts the airflow:

→ Lips together for /b/ and /p/
→ Tongue on teeth for /t/ and /d/
→ Tongue on roof of mouth for /l/ and /n/

Consonants frame the vowels; vowels give words their voice.

2.4

Short Vowel Sounds: The Foundation

Every vowel letter can make more than one sound. The first sound you must master for each vowel is its short sound. Short vowels are produced quickly and cleanly. Tap any row to hear the vowel and its examples.

Mouth Positions

2.6

Voiced vs. Unvoiced Consonants

Try it: Place two fingers on your throat. Say /s/ — no vibration (unvoiced). Now say /z/ — feel the vibration (voiced). This is the only difference between these two sounds.
Voiced — vibration felt
Unvoiced — no vibration

This distinction will become important when you study blends and digraphs in Phase 2.
