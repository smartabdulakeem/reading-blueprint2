8.1
What Is a Phonogram?

A phonogram is a letter or group of letters that consistently represents a particular sound. Phonograms are the repeating patterns of English — the building blocks that appear again and again across thousands of words.

The Big Idea
Why Phonograms Are Powerful
Once you know the phonogram -at, you automatically unlock:
bat, cat, fat, hat, mat, pat, rat, sat, that, flat, brat, splat — and dozens more.

One pattern. Dozens of words. That is the power of phonograms.

8.4 — R-Controlled Vowel Phonograms
The Bossy R

When a vowel is followed by the letter R, the R changes the vowel's sound. The vowel is neither fully short nor fully long — it is pulled toward the R. These are called R-controlled vowels, or the Bossy R.

The Bossy R Rule: When R follows a vowel, the R takes control. The vowel can no longer say its short or long sound — the R bosses it into a new sound entirely.

8.5 — Diphthong Phonograms
Gliding Vowel Sounds

A diphthong is a vowel sound that starts in one position and glides to another within the same syllable. Your mouth actually moves as you say the sound.

Two main diphthongs:
oi / oy — as in oil, coin, boy, toy. Your mouth starts open and glides upward.
ou / ow — as in out, cloud, cow, brown. Your mouth starts open and rounds into a circle.
